How I Learned to Let Go After the First Love

"Some people come into our lives to stay. Others come to change us."

I wrote this years after the breakup, not because the pain disappeared, but because I finally understood it.

I think almost all of us have one first love that shapes us in some way. Some people are lucky and stay with that person until the end of the road. Others have to part ways for many different reasons. I am one of the people who had to say goodbye and I hope the story below can help anyone in the same place feel a little less alone, and maybe find some peace.


1. Why We Broke Up

We were in the same class at university and I was the one who liked her first. She was truly gentle and the kind of person anyone would be lucky to have as a girlfriend. Looking back, I don't think our relationship ended because her mistakes. It ended because I didn't know how to love someone in a healthy way.

I was terrified of losing her. That fear slowly turned into control. I wanted to know what she was doing, who she was with, and I always thought my way was the right way. At the time, I convinced myself it was because I cared. Looking back, I know it came from insecurity.

I also leaned on her to lift my mood, because my feelings dropped very often, so I depended on her completely. I treated her like a place to dump all my frustration. I also depended on her emotionally. Whenever I felt anxious or unhappy, I expected her to make me feel better. Without realizing it, I turned the person I loved into the person responsible for my happiness. I poured my frustration onto her instead of learning how to deal with it myself.

And of course, no one can put up with a boyfriend like that. I wouldn't have wanted to be with someone like that either.


2. The Mistakes I Made After the Breakup

I tried to pull her back. Again, and again, and again. I told myself that if she was gone, I could not keep living. I sent her long messages, the kind where I explained myself, apologised, and then explained myself again. I told myself each message was the last one. None of them were and none of them received a reply. I told myself that I had accepted it, which was not true. I had only run out of ways to reach her.

For a long time, I thought I was simply unable to move on. Years later, I realized that wasn't the whole story. Normally, our own mind learns how to comfort itself. Mine never really did. Somewhere along the way, I had handed that responsibility to her. Whenever I felt anxious, lonely, or unhappy, I turned to her until I felt okay again. Over time, my happiness stopped being something I could create for myself. It became something I expected from another person.

When she was gone, the feeling I used to get from her was gone too, and I went looking for it everywhere, like a person searching for a medicine that does not exist anymore. I call it being addicted to a feeling. I do not know if that is the correct word, but it is the closest one I have.

So I looked for that feeling in other places. I finished a pack of cigarettes just so my mind would stop replaying our conversations. I lie in bed staring at the ceiling until two or three in the morning, hoping that I can sleep. I wake up, reach for my phone, and remember there would be no message from her.

I lived with that pain for so long that it stopped feeling like pain and started feeling like my life. I hid it under work. I hid it under other small pleasures. But I never once looked straight at it, I never said the simple thing out loud: she isn't part of my life anymore.


3. What Finally Helped Me Heal

I ran from the truth for a long time, which meant I never actually dealt with what was going on inside me. I only postponed it. At some point my heart and my mind were simply too tired to keep running. That was the winter I stopped sleeping properly. One night I started listening about Buddhism, then Stoicism came a little after that.

Buddhism taught me that avoiding the truth does not free me from it. I had to look at it and accept it, once and for all. Stoicism taught me something that was harder to hear. My suffering came from the way I saw the breakup. It never came from the breakup itself.

So I practised accepting one fact instead of hiding from it: I am never going to see her again. It was difficult. It was painful. But I think it works the way a tree works. Winter takes the leaves and that is the only way the tree can grow again in spring.

Slowly, I started making small joys for myself. One morning I was walking to lab and I noticed the clouds moving, and for about ten seconds I was not thinking about my pain at all. That was the first time in months. After that I started looking for those ten seconds on purpose. Birds in the morning. The walk home. Small things, and I let them be enough.

The pain is still there. I am not going to tell you it disappears. But now I hold it as a part of my memory instead of a wound I have to cover. And I accept something else too. It was because of her that I finally saw my own faults. Without her, I might have spent my whole life wrapped up in myself and leaning on other people to carry me.


4. If You're Going Through Something Similar

Right after the breakup, do not check up on your ex. Not their social media, not through friends, not by walking past the places they go. Give your heart and your mind a rest. Seeing them only stirs everything up again and it takes away the clear mind you need right now. If you keep looking, you will end up begging them to come back, again and again, and every time you do that you lower your own worth in their eyes. Please do not put your heart through that.

Then look at what went wrong, honestly and calmly. Do not hide from it. Try to see it the way an outsider would see it, not the way it feels at 2am. One day there will be someone else or maybe the same person again, and you do not want to repeat the same mistake with them. Ask yourself the plain question. What was my part in this? If I love someone again, what exactly do I need to do differently? Write the answer down if you have to.

Whatever happens, do not abandon yourself. You still have your family. You still have your work. You still have people around you who would show up if you asked. Do not let one relationship turn you into someone you no longer recognise. That person in the mirror is still worth taking care of, even on the days you do not believe it.

Do not look for someone new until you have actually healed. I believe that when you have grown and when you have taken care of your own wounds, someone will come along who loves you and looks after you. But if you go looking before you are ready, you will only hurt them. They will become the medicine, the same way my ex was my medicine, and that is not fair to anyone. Work on yourself as much as you can, so that when that person arrives, you actually have the strength to love them and care for them properly.

I hope this can help you in some way. Please remember that you are not alone. Take care of your life and your mental health, because the world is so beautiful and no one will die from losing someone.
